My Water-Saving Story

Colleen, Valley Center                             1918

  • I catch rain run-off from my roof in five-gallon buckets and then transfer and store the captured water in 32 gallon trash cans. I use the water to water garden plants.
  • I wash dishes in a plastic basin in the sink and when it almost full, I transfer the water to a two-gallon bucket in my bathtub. That water is then used to flush the toilet.
  • I keep a plastic pitcher nearby to capture water while I am waiting for it warm up. I then transfers that water to the same two-gallon bucket in the tub.
  • I also take only five minute showers and wash only full loads of laundry.

My Water-Saving Story

Beverly, San Diego                             1916

"Ode to My Houseplants" Sprinkle, sprinkle from outside, Helped me water my plants inside. But with no rain in the forecast, I had to find another way, alas! Now trickle, trickle on top of the dirt, I now use ice cubes and they don't hurt. As I'm saving water every day, While just watering my houseplants in another way! Years ago I took my houseplants outside, placed them on the lawn, and drowned them with the hose. This took too long and used excess water. Then, I decided to try a different approach. I placed them into my kitchen sink and watered them with the faucet sprayer. This also was time consuming, and I watched dirt overflow from the pots and disappear down the drain. Then, one day before heading out the door to work and needing to water my plants before I left, I placed ice cubes on the soil of each plant. I had found my houseplant watering solution, and as long as there is a drip pan under each plant, it is not messy. This is a slow drip system that works to this day! Saving water every day, every way!


My Water-Saving Story

Jennifer, San Diego                             1915

Using poster board, we made a GIANT BLUE WATER DROP and posted it on our wall. We involved our family and friends in brainstorming ideas to save water. Everyone was invited to write water-saving ideas on the water drop. It was never too soon to start implementing suggestions! Ideas we put into action right away included: use a broom vs. a hose to clean (patio, walkways); match water level on washer to the size of your load; take five-minute showers; reuse towels if you can do so; use proper size of pans when you cook; don't run the water while rinsing off dishes in the sink; plug tub before turning on the water; keep pitcher of water in the fridge instead of running the tap water (to get it cold); and use leftover water in the kettle to water house plants.  We plan to keep our giant blue water drop on the wall as a reminder to continue to think of water-saving ideas, find ways to implement suggestions, and share our ideas with others so that they can save water, too.

My Water-Saving Story

Karen, Encinitas                             1909

Our beautiful swimming pool is the focal point of our yard, but I know that water is wasted due to evaporation. How much? I did some research and found that In San Diego the water loss averages between 1 and 1.5 inches per week, which means I am loosing over 25,000 gallons every year! A swimming pool cover will greatly reduce evaporation, however I don't want to look at a sheet of plastic or struggle with removing it (our pool is irregularly shaped) every time we use the pool. After a visit to my local pool supply store I discovered that there are products known as liquid pool covers (also called liquid solar covers) which form an invisible micro-thin layer on the water surface to reduce evaporation. With some additional research (note: I am an engineer and enjoy this type of stuff!) I discovered that similar products are used by some water agencies in reservoirs and that evaporation rates are reduced by 30-50%. So by adding just 4 oz. of this product to my pool every week, I will now save an estimated 7,000 to 12,000 gallons of water annually.

My Water-Saving Story

Susanne & Steven, San Diego                             1893

Our rain barrel is filled with precious water every week! In case you are wondering, yes, we do live in San Diego. How do we do it? Instead of waiting for rain, we are capturing the weekly flush of our water purifier. The system is set to flush once a week. In the past, these 60-80 gallons of water were lost as run off. Now we have diverted this water from inside the garage, under a walkway, into our rain barrels. The water is used to hand water many low-water plants and a raised vegetable bed. This allows us to completely turn off a few irrigation stations. All of our turf in the front and back yard was removed earlier this year which makes a huge difference as well. All plants are now on drip irrigation. Our family is proud to make every drop count, every day and every way! As many others do, we also collect water in the shower, cooking liquids, water from using the salad spinner, any leftover water and ice cubes from drinking glasses and use it to water house and patio plants. In addition, we installed low-flow faucet aerators and shower heads throughout the house.

My Water-Saving Story

Joshua, Oceanside                             1884

Our family volunteers as a foster home for a local dog rescue, and we have found that many of the pups enjoy staying cool and splashing in a backyard kiddie pool. However, with water conservation being a growing concern, we stopped filling the pool (much to the dismay of our furry friends). Then our family came up with a solution, thanks to the smart thinking of my 9-year-old daughter, Nova. She commented that a lot of water goes to waste while bringing showers "up to temperature," and she suggested we collect the initial water from a shower into a large bucket, and set it aside to be used elsewhere. When the water in the pool begins to grow algae and needs to be replaced, we scoop it back out of the dogs' pool and find an additional and final use for it in watering our backyard fruit trees. The trees remain healthy and fruitful, the dogs remain happy and cool, and we are saving precious California water at the same time!

My Water-Saving Story

Lee, San Diego                             1880

We save a considerable amount of water and money by using this routine: Before my daily shower, I put a bucket under the tap and turn on just the hot water. It takes about two gallons to get hot. I put the bucket aside and have my shower. I pour the saved two gallons into a watering can and use it on individual plants and trees which saves water and does double duty to save money. There is the initial cost to me of two gallons, however I save the cost of two more gallons that I would have used from the garden hose and I also save the cost of sending the initial two gallons of warm-up water down the sewer. Counting those last two things, figure monthly savings of 60 gals fresh water and 60 gals of wastewater times 12 months, is 720 gals. My wife and I both do this, so it saves water and the cost of 1,440 gallons per year. Think of the savings if thousands of people did this...
